Overview of the Pedagogical Simulation on Aggressive Tax Optimization

International taxation INTPA partner countries Extractive sector Sub-Saharan Africa
1. General Presentation

Base erosion and profit shifting — commonly referred to by the acronym BEPS — represent a structural fiscal challenge for the partner countries of the European Commission's Directorate-General for International Partnerships (INTPA), whose tax revenues rely heavily on the taxation of multinational enterprises operating in the extractive sector. Crivelli, De Mooij, and Keen (2016) estimate that these practices cost developing countries approximately 1.3 percent of GDP annually in foregone revenues, more than twice the estimated impact on advanced economies. This pedagogical simulation, available in French and English versions with identical content, enables participants to engage concretely and interactively with the mechanisms of aggressive tax planning. It is grounded in a stylized case study representative of a mining enterprise operating in a low-income developing country that is a partner of INTPA: the enterprise generates revenues of 300 million euros and, in the baseline scenario, remits 12 million euros in corporate income tax at a statutory rate of 20 percent. A self-assessment quiz completes the learning experience.

2. Structure of the Simulation

The simulation unfolds across three progressive phases. Phase I — Exploration presents nine pre-constructed scenarios covering the principal mechanisms of aggressive tax optimization and their regulatory countermeasures: transfer pricing manipulation, thin capitalization through intra-group financing, commodity price hedging contracts (the "sixth method"), treaty shopping, and their combinations. Corrective scenarios illustrate, symmetrically, the countermeasures drawn from the OECD BEPS Action Plan — the arm's length principle (Actions 8–10), the thin capitalization rule, and the interest limitation rule (Action 4). Phase II — Interactive Simulation invites participants to calibrate their own optimization strategies and observe in real time their effects on the allocation of profit among the host state, the shareholder, and offshore entities. Phase III — Comparative Analysis synthesizes all scenarios in a comparative table accompanied by a BEPS erosion index and a tax policy discussion.

3. Learning Objectives

The simulation pursues three complementary objectives. It first aims to demystify the technical complexity of BEPS mechanisms, making them accessible to a broad range of participants — graduate students, tax administration officials, treaty negotiators, and development practitioners. It then enables participants to quantify the fiscal stakes of aggressive tax optimization in resource-dependent contexts, illuminating the structural vulnerability documented by Cobham and Janský (2018) and by Johannesen, Tørsløv, and Wier (2020). Finally, it cultivates a capacity for evidence-based policy evaluation, allowing participants to experiment with countermeasures recommended by the IMF (2014) and the OECD (2015) and to assess their relative effectiveness across the institutional configurations of INTPA partner countries.

4. Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of a simulation session, participants will have developed the following competencies:

Competency 01 — AnalysisIdentify and decompose the principal mechanisms of fiscal erosion and understand their effects on the tax base of the host state.
Competency 02 — QuantificationCalculate and interpret the budgetary impact of each optimization strategy in terms of revenue losses, offshore profit transfers, and shareholder returns.
Competency 03 — Tax PolicyEvaluate the comparative effectiveness of BEPS countermeasures, accounting for the limited administrative capacity characteristic of low-income countries.
Competency 04 — Analytical PerspectiveAdopt simultaneously the viewpoint of the multinational enterprise and that of the host state, apprehending the information asymmetries inherent in international tax negotiations.
Competency 05 — Documentary LiteracyMobilize academic sources and institutional reports (OECD, IMF, World Bank, European Commission) in connection with sectoral data pertaining to the extractive industries.
Competency 06 — ArgumentationFormulate tax policy recommendations grounded in the quantitative and qualitative analysis of trade-offs between investment attractiveness and domestic revenue mobilization.

These competencies align with the requirements of graduate-level training programs in international taxation and development economics, as well as with the capacity-building needs of tax administrations in INTPA partner countries, including programs coordinated by the African Tax Administration Forum (ATAF) and initiatives conducted under the Platform for Collaboration on Tax (IMF, OECD, UN, World Bank Group).

5. Self-Assessment

A self-assessment quiz completes the pedagogical framework. It covers all mechanisms addressed across the nine scenarios — identification of optimization strategies, interpretation of their fiscal effects, and knowledge of regulatory countermeasures — as well as the conceptual and institutional references mobilized throughout the simulation. Designed as a guided-response instrument with immediate corrective feedback, it may be administered at the conclusion of a session for formative purposes, or prior to a training program for diagnostic placement.
